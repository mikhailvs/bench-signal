Transparency & Reproducibility

Methodology

How Bench Signal collects, processes, links, and analyzes Spokane County Superior Court family law data. Published to support peer review and critical assessment.

1

Case Selection

We targeted 2023 Spokane County Superior Court family law cases (case numbers beginning with 23-, court code 32). The Washington Courts public case viewer was the sole data source — all records are public under RCW 2.28.010.

Case types scraped:

  • Dissolution (type 3) — divorce proceedings with or without children
  • Modification — parenting plan or support modifications
  • Contempt — enforcement and contempt findings
  • Legal Separation — formal separation filings
  • Other Family — paternity, protection orders, miscellaneous

Final corpus: 596 cases, filtered to those with at least one docketed document and an identified judicial officer.

2

Data Collection — Web Scraping

A Playwright-based browser automation pipeline scraped the Washington Courts public portal. Key design decisions:

Browser Pool

Chromium contexts with human-like anti-bot headers (User-Agent, Accept-Language, viewport randomization). No credentials used.

Rate Limiting

Token-bucket limiter with Gaussian jitter (mean 2.3 s, σ 0.8 s between requests). Maximum 3 concurrent contexts.

Search Strategy

Year-prefix enumeration: 23-3-00001-32 through 23-3-05000-32, skipping 404s. Case detail pages fetched individually.

Document Download

PDFs saved to documents/{case_number}/{filename}.pdf. Retry on transient HTTP errors (3× backoff).

Total documents retrieved: ~17,645 across all cases. Scraping ran over three nights in early 2024.

3

PDF Processing & OCR

Text extraction uses a 3-pass cascade to maximize coverage:

  1. Pass 1 pdfplumber — extracts embedded text from digitally-created PDFs (court-generated orders). Covers ~35% of documents cleanly.
  2. Pass 2 PyMuPDF (fitz) — fallback for PDFs with partial text layers (form-filled documents where fields are images).
  3. Pass 3 Tesseract OCR — renders each PDF page as a 300 DPI JPEG, then runs Tesseract 5 with the eng+legal tessdata. Applied to all documents yielding <50 chars of embedded text per page.

OCR Accuracy Caveat

Handwritten judicial annotations and stamped text in scanned documents have estimated 78–82% character accuracy. Form-filled PDF fields where the fill layer is rasterized score 85–91%. Pure typewritten documents score 96%+. These errors propagate to phrase extraction; treat TF-IDF phrases from low-OCR documents as approximate.

4

Motion-Outcome Linking

Linking a motion document to its ruling is the hardest step. Docket entries lack explicit motion-order relationships. We use a 5-pass linking strategy:

Pass A — Dispositive Document Match

Documents whose docket title contains "Order on" or "Ruling on" are matched to the most recent motion of the same type filed within 90 days. Covers ~2,600 granted outcomes. Highest precision (~97%). Excluded from ML training to prevent label leakage.

Pass B — Title Keyword Match

Order text matched against pending motions by motion type keyword and outcome language ("GRANTED", "DENIED"). Generic order titles ("ORDER ON") fall back to reading order text for outcome. Covers ~164 links.

Pass C — Sub-Number Proximity

Sequentially-numbered documents (e.g., 045→046) where 046 contains outcome language are linked to 045. Covers ~324 links.

Pass D — Date Proximity

Unlinked motions matched to orders filed within 60 days with compatible motion types. Confidence threshold 0.45. Covers ~335 links.

Pass E — VRP Statement

VRP (Verbatim Report of Proceedings) court speech analyzed for ruling statements ("motion is granted/denied"). Covers ~31 links.

Coverage: 3,426 of ~7,881 motions are linked (43%). The primary bottleneck is ambiguous docket titles and motions without corresponding order documents. The linker is designed to be conservative — false positives are more harmful than missed links. 1,438 links (contested outcomes) are used for judge-level analysis.

5

NLP & Feature Extraction

TF-IDF Phrase Lift

Bigrams and trigrams extracted from granted and denied motion text. Lift = TF-IDF score in one class divided by frequency in the other. High-lift phrases indicate language patterns that distinguish outcomes. Stopwords filtered with a legal domain extension.

RCW Citation Extraction

Regex pattern RCW\s+\d+\.\d+[\.\d]* extracts Washington Revised Code citations. Mean citation counts per page are compared between granted and denied motions.

Sentiment (VADER)

VADER sentiment with a legal-domain lexicon patch (e.g., "contempt" shifted to negative). Applied to VRP transcript segments. Not yet used in grant-rate prediction.

Embeddings

legal-bert (sentence-transformers) encodes motion text for semantic similarity search. Powers future "find similar motions" feature. Not used in current grant-rate stats.

6

Statistical Methods

  • Mann-Whitney U Non-parametric test for differences in page count, word count, and RCW count distributions between granted and denied motions. Used because distributions are right-skewed and sample sizes are unequal.
  • Gradient Boosting XGBoost classifier trained on per-motion features (word count, page count, RCW count, days before hearing, motion type, case subtype). Evaluated by AUC-ROC with 5-fold cross-validation. Sample size constraints limit reliability for rare motion types.
  • SHAP TreeSHAP explanations for feature importance in the gradient boosting model. Confirms that motion type and case subtype dominate over textual features given current data volume.
  • Weather Correlation NOAA Spokane daily summaries joined to hearing dates. Condition categories (rainy, dry, cold, hot, snowy, normal) defined by NWS thresholds. No significant correlation found (all p > 0.4).
!

Limitations & Caveats

  • ▸ Partial linking coverage (43%). 57% of motions cannot be linked to an outcome due to ambiguous docket titles or missing order documents. Results reflect only linkable documents, which may be systematically different (e.g., better-formatted attorney-filed motions).
  • ▸ No attorney data. Attorney quality, experience, and firm are major confounders not captured in docket records. Judge-level patterns may partly reflect systematic differences in who appears before each judge.
  • ▸ OCR errors. Form-filled and handwritten documents have significant character error rates. Phrase extraction from these documents is unreliable.
  • ▸ Single year snapshot. 2023 only. Personnel changes, caseload shifts, and procedural reforms are not captured. Grant rates for judges with few outcomes (<10) are statistically unreliable.
  • ▸ Uncontested motions inflated. Many "granted" outcomes are default judgments or stipulated orders — both parties agreed. These inflate grant rates and are indistinguishable from contested grants in docket data alone.
  • ▸ Grant rates include all linked outcomes. Ministerial filings (fee waivers, uncontested defaults, summons service) are not separated from contested motions in the current data, which inflates the overall grant rate substantially. Figures should not be interpreted as judicial decision rates.
  • ▸ TF-IDF phrase analysis and machine learning predictions are currently suppressed pending larger samples. The negative class (denied motions, n=46 total) is too small for statistically reliable signal detection.
  • ▸ Grant rates include all linked outcomes, including ministerial filings. Contested-motion-only filtering is applied where indicated.
  • ▸ The motion prep checklist is based on small samples (n<100 contested outcomes with complete features). All findings are directional associations, not predictions.
  • ▸ Not legal advice. This analysis is for academic and journalistic research. It does not constitute legal advice and should not be relied upon for case strategy.
